## 1. The thesis

**swift-io is the IO substrate**: one strategy-agnostic witness, three
handler families, no framework dependency. It encodes an algebraic
effect theory of IO in the Plotkin–Power–Pretnar sense — but as a
*property of the design*, not a framework to import.

The public algebra Σ_IO is four operations on the `IO` witness
(`Sources/IO Core/IO.swift:133`):

```
read  : (borrowing FD, Memory.Buffer.Mutable) async throws(IO.Error) → Int
write : (borrowing FD, Memory.Buffer)         async throws(IO.Error) → Int
close : (consuming FD)                         async                  → Void
ready : (borrowing FD, Kernel.Event.Interest) async throws(IO.Error) → Void
```

Plus one scheduling field (`unownedExecutor`) — topology metadata,
not an IO operation. Strategy is chosen at construction; consumers
see only the witness.

## 2. Composition (the substrate point)

Higher-level subsystems extend Σ_IO by signature coproduct, then
discharge the extension to swift-io's witness:

- `swift-file-system` will add `{open, seek, stat, …}` and reduce them
  to `IO.read` / `IO.write` / `IO.close` / blocking syscalls.
- `swift-server` will add `{accept, connect, send, recv, …}` and reduce
  them to `IO.ready` + `IO.read` / `IO.write` / `IO.close`.

This is what makes swift-io "the substrate". Consumers depend on the
witness type, not on a strategy. They get host-adaptive IO via
`IO.default()` for free, or pin a specific strategy when needed.

## 3. Encoding — single, settled

The witness is generated by the `@Witness` macro on `IO` (see the
`@Witness` annotation at `Sources/IO Core/IO.swift:132`). This is the
**dictionary encoding** of algebraic effects, in the Leijen–Koka
tradition:

- **Signature** = `IO` struct of closures — capability
  (Brachthaeuser).
- **Handler** = `IO.{Blocking, Event, Completion}.Actor` — runner
  (Ahman & Bauer 2020, *Runners in Action*, ESOP). Cited in
  `IO.swift:128–131`.

There is no parallel free encoding in current code. The
`Experiments/io-algebra/` experiment is one direction-finding
investigation, not a co-equal track. If a free encoding ever ships,
this section gets revised; do not preemptively split docs.

## 4. What this commits to and excludes

**Committed:**
- First-order operations, single-resume handlers (compatible with
  Swift `async/await`).
- Compositional discharge by reduction (consumer handlers reduce to
  Σ_IO; Σ_IO handlers reduce to platform syscalls).
- Three handler families: Blocking, Events, Completions; host-adaptive
  fallback via `IO.default()`.

**Excluded:**
- Multi-shot resumption (would need delimited continuations; Swift
  lacks them).
- Scoped operations (Wu/Schrijvers/Hinze 2014); operations are flat.
- Effect-system dependency. swift-io stands alone.
- Per-strategy public witness types (intermediate `IO.Event.Driver`
  etc.); collapsed into the unified `IO`.

**Deferred (not excluded):**
- Stream layer (`IO.Reader`/`IO.Writer`); listed in `perfect-api.md`
  Tier 0 as planned; not implemented.
- `~Copyable` resources × algebraic-effects literature (Fu et al.
  OOPSLA 2020; not surveyed).
- Windows IOCP handler family.
- Concurrent effects across tasks.

## 5. Open work

1. The free ↔ dictionary equivalence is folklore in the
   algebraic-effects literature; if/when a free encoding ships, locate
   or write the proof. Until then, not load-bearing.
2. Σ_IO's law set (E_IO) is informal; foundation §7 has draft
   equations. Property tests against the three handler families are
   the natural next step when a real consumer needs the guarantee.
3. Windows IOCP handler — `IO.completions(on:)` is Linux-only today.
